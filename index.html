What is Electricity?

Electricity is the flow of electric charge, typically carried by electrons in a conductor like a wire. It powers our homes, devices, and much of modern life.

At its core, electricity involves the movement of electrons, which are negatively charged particles in atoms. When electrons move from one place to another, they create an electric current.

Basic Concepts

  • Electric Charge: Property of matter that causes it to experience force in an electromagnetic field. Electrons have negative charge.
  • Current (I): The rate of flow of electric charge, measured in Amperes (A).
  • Voltage (V): The electric potential difference, like "pressure" pushing the current, measured in Volts (V).
  • Resistance (R): Opposition to the flow of current, measured in Ohms (Ω).

Diagram: Simple Circuit (Battery connected to a Lamp)

Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law states that the current (I) through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage (V) across it and inversely proportional to the resistance (R): I = V / R. It can also be rearranged to V = I * R or R = V / I.

Types of Circuits

Series Circuit: Components connected end-to-end; current is the same through all, but voltage divides.

Parallel Circuit: Components connected across the same voltage; voltage is the same, but current divides.
